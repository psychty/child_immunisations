Coverage of childhood vaccinations

This analyses presents the latest available data on the coverage of routine childhood vaccinations (from birth to age 5) in primary care settings as well as influenza and HPV school-based vaccination programmes in West Sussex.

Population vaccination coverage is the best indicator of the level of protection a population will have against vaccine preventable communicable diseases and coverage is highly correlated with levels of disease. As such, keeping a close eye on changes in vaccination coverage helps to identify possible drops in immunity before levels of disease rise. The minimum recommended coverage for vaccination is 95% of the population.

Good to know...

Data collections are quality assured at the time of collection by UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) and further validation is carried out by NHS Digital prior to publication (detailed here - https://digital.nhs.uk/data-and-information/publications/statistical/nhs-immunisation-statistics/england---2020-21/appendices). Cover of vaccination evaluated rapidly (COVER) data is provided by the Child Health Information Service (CHIS) providers and is reported in the public domain quarterly and annually.

Importantly, the GP data are experimental and should be treated with caution. It is not an official statistic. Practices with fewer than five eligible patients in a release of data are suppressed. Changes to geographies (such as GP practice closures, mergers, new sites and changes to PCNs) can also impact on estimates.

There are also sometimes changes to the vaccinations offered. For example, the diphtheria, tetanus, acellular pertussis, polio, Haemophilus influenzae type b (DTaP/IPV/Hib), known as the 5-in-1 vaccine, was replaced by the DTaP/IPV/Hib/HepB (6-in-1) vaccine in August 2017.

Schedule of immunisations; 12 months

The combined DTaP IPV Hib (DTaP/IPV/Hib/HepB), known as the 6 in 1 vaccine, is the first in a course of vaccines offered to babies to protect them against diphtheria, pertussis (whooping cough), tetanus, Haemophilus influenzae type b (an important cause of childhood meningitis and pneumonia), hepititis B, nd polio (IPV is inactivated polio vaccine).

The Pneuomcoccal conjulate vaccine (PCV) vaccine protects against pneumococcal infections that can cause pneumonia, septicaemia or meningitis.

The Meningitis B (MenB) vaccine protects against invasive meningococcal disease capsule group B most commonly presenting as either septicaemia or meningitis, or a combination of both. The vaccine was introduced into the routine childhood immunisation programme in September 2015 for babies at 8 and 16 weeks of age with a booster dose afther the first birthday.

The rotavirus vaccine protects against gastroenteritis. Rotavirus vaccine is offered to children aged from 6 weeks up to 23 weeks.

The DTaP/IPV/Hib/HepB, PCV and MenB vaccines are available to children at any point in their first year and by the time a child reaches their first birthday they should have received their third DTaP/IPV/Hib/HepB, PCV, MenB and rotavirus vaccines.

Seasonal Flu (Influenzae) vaccinations; School-aged children September to January 2018/19 to September to January 2022/23;

Influenza (also known as Flu) is a highly infectious viral illness spread by droplet infection. The flu vaccination is usually offered to people who are at greater risk of developing serious complications if they catch flu. However, extending the childhood influenza vaccination programme to healthy children provides direct protection for those immunised as well as indirectly protecting the remaining population by reducing transmission.

Coverage data reflects all eligible children in school years reception to year 11 that have been vaccinated whether the vaccination took place in school, by GPs, or in pharmacies. Figures are provisional and may be incomplete due to the nature of the systems used to obtain data returns, which varies from provider to provider. Local NHS England teams and their providers submitted aggregate LA level vaccination data manually to PHE via the ImmForm website.

In each seasonal flu vaccination programme (e.g. September - January), target year groups are announced for prioritising vaccination due to capacity and supply issues. In recent years additional year groups have been added to the priority list.

The COVID-19 pandemic had a big impact on the seasonal flu vaccination programme for a number of reasons.

As such, for some years there are no data for some year groups and/or uptake is very low.

In 2021 to 2022, the national ambition was to achieve at least 70% vaccine uptake in school aged children in Reception to Year 6 (age 4 to 11 years old). This has not been achieved locally or nationally.

Human papillomavirus (HPV); school aged children

The adolescent HPV immunisation programme is a school-based programme, with alternative provision in place for children who are not in mainstream schools. Girls and boys aged 12 to 13 years (born after 1 September 2006) are offered the human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccine. In England, girls and boys aged 12 to 13 years are routinely offered the 1st HPV vaccination when they're in school Year 8. The 2nd dose is offered 6 to 24 months after the 1st dose. It is important to have both doses of the vaccine to be properly protected.

HPV vaccine coverage in 2021 to 2022 is still not back up to pre-pandemic levels and there is significant regional and local variation. Some of the reported reasons for this are listed here:

  • Lower attendance rates in schools during high COVID-19 incidence periods.
  • Challenges due to offer of COVID-19 vaccines for 12 to 15 year olds in secondary schools in the 2021 to 2022 academic year.
  • Reduced consent return rates
  • Vaccine hesitancy
  • Reduced capacity of vaccine administrators due to additional pressure of flu vaccine programmes during the autumn term.
